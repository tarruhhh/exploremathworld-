2. Geometry


Euclidean Geometry

  • Points, Lines, Angles: Basic elements of geometry.
  • Shapes: Two-dimensional figures like triangles, squares, circles.
  • Properties: Relationships between sides, angles, and areas of shapes.

    • - Example: The Pythagorean Theorem states that in a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (c) is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (a and b).
    - Formula: a² + b² = c²
    - Example: In a right triangle with sides of length 3 and 4, the hypotenuse has length 5 because 3² + 4² = 5².


    Coordinate Geometry

    • Coordinate Systems: Use coordinates (like the Cartesian plane) to represent geometric figures.

      • - Example: The point (2, 3) is located 2 units to the right and 3 units up from the origin on the Cartesian plane.
    • Distance Formula: Calculates the distance between two points in the coordinate plane.

      • - Formula: √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]
      - Example: The distance between the points (1, 2) and (4, 6) is: √[(4 - 1)² + (6 - 2)²] = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5.
    • Slope: Measures the steepness of a line.
      - Formula: (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)
      - Example: The slope of the line passing through (1, 2) and (4, 6) is: (6 - 2) / (4 - 1) = 4 / 3.
    • Equation of a Line: Represents a line in the coordinate plane.
      - Formula (Slope-intercept form): y = mx + c (m is the slope, c is the y-intercept)
      - Example: The equation of a line with a slope of 2 and a y-intercept of -1 is y = 2x - 1.

    Transformations

    • Transformations: Move, resize, or reflect shapes.

      • - Types:
      • Translation: Shifting a shape without changing its size or orientation.
      • Example: Moving a triangle 3 units to the right and 2 units up.
      • Rotation: Turning a shape around a fixed point.
      • Example: Rotating a square 90 degrees counterclockwise about its center.
      • Reflection: Flipping a shape across a line.
      • Example: Reflecting a triangle across the y-axis.

      Solid Geometry

      • Three-Dimensional Shapes: Cubes, spheres, cones, cylinders, pyramids.
      • Volume: The amount of space a 3D object occupies.
      • Formulas:
        • Cube: Volume = side length x side length x side length
        • Example: A cube with sides of 4 cm has a volume of 4 x 4 x 4 = 64 cubic cm.
        • Rectangular Prism: Volume = length x width x height
        • Example: A rectangular prism with length 5 cm, width 3 cm, and height 2 cm has a volume of 5 x 3 x 2 = 30 cubic cm.
        • Sphere: Volume = (4/3)πr³ (where r is the radius)
        • Example: A sphere with a radius of 3 cm has a volume of (4/3)π(3³) = 36π cubic cm.
        • Cone: Volume = (1/3)πr²h (where r is the radius, h is the height)
        • Example: A cone with a radius of 2 cm and a height of 5 cm has a volume of (1/3)π(2²)(5) = (20/3)π cubic cm.
        • Cylinder: Volume = πr²h (where r is the radius, h is the height)
        • Example: A cylinder with a radius of 3 cm and a height of 7 cm has a volume of π(3²)(7) = 63π cubic cm.
        • Pyramid: Volume = (1/3)Bh (where B is the area of the base, h is the height)
        • Example: A square pyramid with a base side length of 6 cm and a height of 4 cm has a volume of (1/3)(6²)(4) = 48 cubic cm.

        Trigonometry


        Trigonometric Functions: Sine, cosine, and tangent (sin, cos, tan) relate angles and sides of right triangles.
        - SOH CAH TOA: Mnemonic for remembering the ratios:
        • Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse
        • Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
        • Tangent = Opposite / Adjacent
          - Example: In a right triangle with an angle of 30 degrees, the opposite side has length 5 cm and the hypotenuse has length 10 cm.
        • sin(30°) = 5/10 = 1/2
        • cos(30°) = √3/2
        • tan(30°) = 1/√3 = √3/3

          - Trigonometric Identities: Equations that are true for all values of the variables involved.
        • Example: sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 is a fundamental trigonometric identity.
        • Example: Using the identity, if sin(θ) = 3/5, we can find cos(θ): cos²(θ) = 1 - sin²(θ) = 1 - (3/5)² = 16/25 cos(θ) = ±√(16/25) = ±4/5 (the sign of cos(θ) depends on the quadrant of θ).
          - Graphs of Trigonometric Functions: Trigonometric functions have periodic graphs, meaning they repeat over a regular interval.
        • Example: The graph of the sine function oscillates between -1 and 1, with a period of 2π.
          - Inverse Trigonometric Functions: Find the angle corresponding to a given trigonometric ratio.
        • Example: arcsin(0.5) = 30° because sin(30°) = 0.5.
          - Applications of Trigonometry: Used in navigation, surveying, engineering, and physics.
        • Example: Trigonometry is used to calculate distances, heights, and angles in surveying and construction.
        • Example: In navigation, trigonometry is used to determine the position of ships and airplanes.
